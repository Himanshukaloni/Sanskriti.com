Kasar Devi Temple

Kasar Devi Temple is a famous spiritual site located near Almora in Uttarakhand, dedicated to Goddess Kasar Devi, a form of Durga. The temple is believed to date back to the 2nd century CE and is set on a hilltop that offers panoramic views of the Himalayas and Almora town.

  • The place is known for its serene environment and mystical energy, as it lies on the Van Allen Belt, believed to have strong geomagnetic fields.
  • It became popular during the Hippie movement of the 1960s and 70s, attracting personalities like Swami Vivekananda, Bob Dylan, and many seekers.
  • Every year, the Kasar Devi Fair (Mela) is held during Kartik Purnima, drawing devotees and tourists alike.

OM Parvat

OM Parvat is a sacred mountain located in the Dharchula district of Uttarakhand, near the India-Nepal-Tibet border. What makes it unique is the natural snow formation on the mountain that resembles the sacred "‡•ê (Om)" symbol, visible especially during clear weather.

  • It holds immense spiritual and religious significance for Hindus and is considered a divine place.
  • Om Parvat lies on the route of the Kailash Mansarovar Yatra and Adi Kailash Yatra, making it an important pilgrimage stop.
  • Surrounded by the majestic Himalayas, the site offers breathtaking views and a deeply peaceful atmosphere.

Adi Kalash

Adi Kalash, also known as Chhota Kailash, is a sacred mountain located in the Pithoragarh district of Uttarakhand, near the Indo-Tibet border. It is revered as an abode of Lord Shiva, similar to Mount Kailash in Tibet, and is considered one of the holiest pilgrimage sites in the Himalayas.

  • The mountain resembles Mount Kailash and holds deep spiritual significance for Hindus.
  • Nearby lies the serene Parvati Sarovar (lake) and Gauri Kund, adding to its sacredness.
  • Adi Kalash is part of the Kailash-Mansarovar Yatra route, but it can also be visited separately as the Adi Kailash Yatra.
  • Surrounded by snow-capped peaks, forests, and rivers, it is a place of both natural beauty and devotion.
